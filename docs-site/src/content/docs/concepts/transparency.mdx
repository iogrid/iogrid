The original sin of residential proxy networks was Hola: a free VPN that
silently sold its users' bandwidth to Bright Data with no consent and no
visibility. iogrid is the deliberate opposite — categorical opt-in consent at
install time, and a live audit dashboard that shows providers exactly what
their IP carries.

<Aside type="note" title="Why this page exists">
  Every other section of these docs explains what iogrid **does**. This one
  explains what we let providers **see** — the single biggest differentiator
  versus every incumbent.
</Aside>

## The provider audit dashboard

Open the local control UI at [http://localhost:7777](http://localhost:7777)
or the hosted view at `dashboard.iogrid.org/provide` after linking.

The dashboard shows, for each provider, in real time and historically:

### Per-request stream

- Timestamp · destination domain · category label · bytes in · bytes out
- Customer ID (aggregated — `cus_abc123` only, never email or company name)
- Whether the request was passed, blocked, rate-limited, or rerouted

### Category rollups

Every byte is labeled with one of the well-defined categories:

| Category | What it means | Default opt-in |
|---|---|---|
| `ecommerce-monitor` | Price / availability / SKU scraping of public storefronts | ✅ |
| `seo-serp` | Search-engine result-page scraping | ✅ |
| `ad-verify` | Verifying ad placements / brand-safety crawls | ✅ |
| `travel-aggregate` | Flight, hotel, car-rental price aggregation | ✅ |
| `lead-gen` | Public business contact info scraping | ✅ |
| `ai-training-data` | Public-web crawling for AI training corpora | ✅ |
| `social-intel` | Social-media platform scraping | 🟡 opt-in |
| `adult` | Adult-content destinations | 🟡 opt-in |
| `banking` | Bank, fintech, money-services domains | 🟡 opt-in + KYC |
| `government` | `.gov`, `.mil`, public-sector | ❌ blocked |
| `csam-suspect` | Hash matches against NCMEC / INTERPOL | ❌ blocked |
| `other` | Doesn't match any taxonomy bucket | ✅ |

The category is decided **pre-flight** by the coordinator (so blocking happens
before the byte ever leaves the provider). Providers see the category label
on every entry of their audit stream.

### One-click controls

- **Block this destination forever** — adds a regex match to the provider's
  personal blocklist. Scoped to the provider, doesn't affect anyone else.
- **Block this category** — opts the provider out of that whole category.
- **Block this customer** — refuses any future scheduling to that customer ID.
- **Drain me now** — stops new workloads immediately. In-flight workloads
  finish naturally.

### Historical export

- 24-hour rolling view in the UI.
- 90-day audit log retained server-side (legal / compliance requirement).
- CSV / JSONL export available per provider, at any time, no questions asked.

## Real-time anomaly alerts

Several alerts fire automatically and surface in the dashboard + via email:

- "Your IP just served LinkedIn for 8 minutes" — happens when a customer
  session lingers on a hot destination. One-click block destination.
- "Unusual destination diversity" — when a new customer pattern is detected.
- "Pre-flight blocks elevated" — when more than the usual share of requests
  are being refused (could indicate a malicious customer trying to abuse you).
- "Quality score dropped" — uptime / latency / success-rate regression.

## What providers do **not** see

For privacy reasons (and to honor customer contracts), some things are
**deliberately** hidden from providers:

- Customer's full identity (only their abstract `cus_*` ID)
- The customer's HTTPS payload (the coordinator does not decrypt customer TLS;
  the provider sees encrypted bytes only)
- Other providers' traffic
- The customer's billing or address info

## Why this is also our legal defense

Aggressive pre-flight filtering + per-provider categorical opt-in + live
audit log + 90-day server-side retention is what lets us argue providers are
**passive intermediaries with informed consent**. It's not just a marketing
posture; it's the difference between a defensible position and the position
that got William Weber convicted for running a Tor exit relay with no
filtering and no commercial intermediary.
